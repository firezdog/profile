Why I Program

My first introduction to the computer was games; my first introduction to programming was DOS, which my dad had to tinker with interminably before I could get a chance to play them. In high school, I learned Java and C in the hopes of programming my own games -- but the complexity of the task was daunting, and I turned to writing as as a more accessible method of developing my fantasies.

Writing introduced me to the life of the mind. My role-models became authors such as James Joyce, Dante, Rimbaud, Virgil -- and then Kant. My interests expanded beyond literature: in graduate school I studied philosophy and tried to pick up some of the basics of math, physics, and biology. I wanted to understand how things worked, how to analyze and solve difficult problems: I wanted to learn what people with big ideas had to say, and I wanted to be able to own those ideas.

I've always been driven by my interests and am willing to sacrifice everything to pursue them. That's why when, after five years of graduate school, I became obsessed with Chinese, I decided to leave my studies and go to China. I spent five years there learning the language and culture, meanwhile teaching English to support myself. At the end of that time I was conversationally fluent and could read and write (I even taught myself to write some calligraphy), but I wasn't sure teaching was my passion. I decided it was time to go back to the US and embark on a career.

Since my return, I have worked in law, translation, and data-entry, but I've found these fields are either too routine or too inaccessible to satisfy my need for intellectual engagement. A family friend suggested that, given my analytical bent and my facility with computers, programming might be a good area to explore. I started to learn Python; the journey since then has been deep and rewarding.

I am now working as hard as I can to improve my knowledge and build projects. In February of 2018, I enrolled at Coding Dojo, where I've been learning front and back-end web development in Python, C#, and MEAN. After several months of intensive study, I am ready to take my first steps into the field.
